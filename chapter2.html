Section 1

Introduction: Why Talk About Licenses?

In Chapter 1, we focused on freedom, history, and philosophy. In this chapter, we look at the legal side. Every serious software project, free or proprietary, has a license. The license tells you what you are allowed to do and what you are not allowed to do.

A software license is a legal agreement between the author of the software and the user. When you install software, even if you click "Next" without reading anything, you are accepting its license. As future engineers, you must understand basic ideas so that you do not break the law or the ethical rules of your profession.

home

House Analogy

Think of software like a house. The license is a document that says who can enter, under which conditions, what they can do inside, and what they cannot do.

  • 🚪 Running the program
  • 📋 Copying it
  • 📤 Sharing it
  • 🔧 Modifying it
  • 🔗 Using parts in other programs
Section 2

Copyright Basics for Software

What is Copyright?

Copyright is a legal system that gives the author of a creative work special rights over that work. These rights usually include:

  • The right to copy the work
  • The right to distribute it
  • The right to modify it
  • The right to create new works based on it

When you write code, even a small program, you are the author and you have copyright on that code. You do not need to register anything. The rights are automatic.

How Copyright Applies to Software

For software, copyright covers:

  • The source code
  • The compiled binary (executable)
  • Documentation, logos, icons, and other resources

By default, "all rights reserved" means nobody is allowed to copy, modify, or share your program without your permission. A software license is the way you give this permission in a clear and explicit way.

License as a Permission

A license is not a sale of the program. When you install a program, you usually do not "own" the software code. You only receive permission to use it under certain conditions.

Proprietary License — Allows:

  • ✅ Install on one computer
  • ✅ Use for personal/professional work

Forbids:

  • ❌ Copy for friends
  • ❌ Reverse engineer
  • ❌ Modify

Free Software License — Guarantees:

  • ✅ Run for any purpose
  • ✅ Study how it works
  • ✅ Redistribute copies
  • ✅ Share modifications
Section 3

Proprietary Licenses

Characteristics of Proprietary Licenses

A proprietary license keeps almost all control in the hands of the software publisher. Typical rules include:

  • You can only install on a limited number of devices, sometimes only one.
  • You cannot share copies with others, even if they are your classmates or colleagues.
  • You cannot access or modify the source code.
  • You cannot use the software for some purposes (e.g., no commercial use with a student version).
  • You may not be allowed to use the software in certain countries or for certain activities.

Updates, Support, and Lock-in

Proprietary licenses are usually connected to a business model:

  • The company sells licenses or subscriptions.
  • You may have to pay extra for updates or support.
  • If you stop paying, you may lose access or updates.
warning Vendor Lock-in
You depend on one company for bug fixes, new features, file formats. If the company changes prices, closes its service, or stops supporting your version — you may be forced to upgrade or migrate in a hurry.

Impact on Institutions and Users

For universities, public administrations, and companies, proprietary licenses can create problems:

  • High costs for many seats (hundreds or thousands of users)
  • Legal risks if some copies are not properly licensed
  • Difficulty in adapting the software to local needs or languages
  • Long-term dependence on one foreign vendor

These concerns are especially important for developing countries like Algeria, which must manage budgets carefully and protect their digital sovereignty.

Section 4

Free and Open Source Licenses

Free and open source licenses are designed to give you more control. They are based on the four freedoms:

  • Run the program for any purpose
  • Study how it works
  • Modify it
  • Share copies of the original or modified version

Within FOSS licenses, we can identify two big families:

sync

Copyleft Licenses

Examples: GPL, LGPL, AGPL

Require that modified versions remain free when distributed.

lock_open

Permissive Licenses

Examples: MIT, BSD, Apache

Allow almost anything. Modified versions may become proprietary.

Section 5

Copyleft Licenses: Keeping Software Free

personFirst author gives freedom
arrow_forward
buildYou improve the software
arrow_forward
publicShare with same freedom

Copyleft uses copyright in a smart way. The author says: "You can use, modify, and share my software, but if you share it, you must keep it free for others too."

Copyleft does not force you to share your private modifications if you only use them inside your organization and do not distribute the software. The strong obligation appears when you distribute copies to others.

The GNU General Public License (GPL)

The GNU GPL is the most famous copyleft license. Many important projects use it, such as the Linux kernel and many GNU tools.

✅ Use the software for any purpose
✅ Must provide source code when distributing
✅ Modified versions must also be under the GPL
❌ Cannot add extra restrictions that remove user freedoms
push_pin Example
If you take a GPL text editor, add features, and give copies of your modified version to students, you must also provide the source code and allow others to modify your version under the GPL.

The GNU Lesser GPL (LGPL)

The LGPL is a weaker form of copyleft, often used for libraries. It allows proprietary programs to use the library without being forced to be GPL, as long as some technical conditions are respected (e.g., allowing users to replace the library with a modified version).

lightbulb Example
A graphical library under LGPL can be used in both free and proprietary applications, which makes it attractive for many developers.

Copyleft vs Copyright

It is important to understand that copyleft and copyright are not opposites. Copyleft depends on copyright.

copyright

Gives the author exclusive right to control copying, modification, and distribution.

Usually: "All rights reserved"

A way an author uses copyright power to guarantee freedom for all users.

"Some rights reserved — freedom preserved"

Without copyright, anyone could take your code and make it proprietary. Copyleft prevents this.

Section 6

Permissive Licenses: More Flexibility

What is a Permissive License?

Permissive licenses allow almost anything, as long as you keep a simple notice of the original authors and license. They do not require that modified versions remain free software.

MIT
description

Very short and simple. Use, copy, modify, distribute freely. Keep original copyright notice. No warranty by authors.

Permissive
BSD
article

Similar to MIT. Multiple variants (2-clause, 3-clause). Can use in proprietary software. Keep copyright notice.

Permissive
Apache 2.0
verified

Permissive with patent provisions. Gives users a patent license from contributors. Used by large projects and companies.

Permissive + Patents

You can take MIT or BSD licensed code and include it in proprietary software, without the obligation to release your own code.

Section 7

Copyleft vs Permissive: A Quick Comparison

Aspect 🔄 Copyleft (GPL, LGPL) 🔓 Permissive (MIT, BSD, Apache)
Modified versions Must remain free Can become proprietary
User freedoms Protected strongly Gives more flexibility to developers
Best for Building a common pool of free software Libraries and tools used in many contexts
Commercial use May be harder to mix with proprietary code Easy to integrate into commercial products
explore Which to choose?
  • If your main goal is to protect user freedoms → choose copyleft like GPL
  • If you want your code used everywhere including proprietary products → choose permissive like MIT or Apache
Section 8

License Compatibility

What is License Compatibility?

License compatibility answers: "Can I combine code under License A with code under License B in one project?"

If the conditions of the two licenses can be respected together, they are compatible. If not, they are incompatible.

✅
MIT + GPL → Compatible

MIT is permissive and does not conflict with GPL rules. MIT code can be included in GPL projects.

❌
GPL + Proprietary → Incompatible

The GPL requires that the whole combined work be GPL when distributed. This conflicts with proprietary restrictions.

Why Students Should Care

As students, you may reuse code from the internet, publish projects, or collaborate on FOSS projects. Understanding license compatibility helps you avoid legal and ethical problems and teaches you to respect other developers' work.

Section 9

Implications for Algerian Educational Institutions and Businesses

account_balance

For Universities & Schools

  • Budget: Using FOSS reduces spending on licenses. Saved money → better hardware, internet, training.
  • Equality: When tools are free, every student can install the same software at home.
  • Academic Freedom: Access to source code allows study of real-world systems, not just theory.
  • Local Adaptation: Universities can adapt software to Arabic, Tamazight, and local needs without a foreign vendor.

At the University of Tipaza, using FOSS in this course makes practical work easier — every student can have the tools on their personal laptop.

business

For Businesses & Startups

  • Lower Entry Barriers: Startups can begin with FOSS tools instead of expensive proprietary solutions.
  • Control & Independence: Not tied to one vendor. Hire local developers or companies to maintain systems.
  • Export Potential: Contribute to international projects and build products for local and African markets.
  • Compliance: Using legally clean FOSS reduces legal risks, especially with international partners.
public

Strategic National Perspective

Encouraging the correct use of FOSS licenses in Algeria can strengthen digital sovereignty, support the local IT ecosystem, and build trust with international partners. For a country that wants to develop its knowledge economy, understanding software licenses is not a detail — it is a strategic choice.

Section 10

Conclusion of Chapter 2

In this chapter, we saw that:

  • Software licenses are essential rules that describe what you can and cannot do with software.
  • Proprietary licenses restrict copying, modification, and sharing, and can create vendor lock-in and high long-term costs.
  • Free and open source licenses are built around the four freedoms, and are divided into copyleft and permissive families.
  • Copyleft licenses like the GPL protect user freedoms by requiring that modified versions remain free; permissive licenses like MIT, BSD, and Apache allow more flexibility.
  • Copyleft is built on copyright, and uses it to protect freedom instead of restricting it.
  • License compatibility matters when combining code from different sources.
  • For Algerian educational institutions and businesses, the right license choices reduce costs, increase independence, and support local innovation.

As future engineers in Algeria, understanding these legal and ethical aspects will guide your choices in projects, startups, and professional work. You will be better prepared to build solutions that are both technically strong and legally sound.
